# Lean Canvas — block-by-block guidance

Detail reference for the `lean-canvas` skill. The Lean Canvas is Ash Maurya's adaptation of Osterwalder's Business Model Canvas (see `../../../reference/methodology.md`). Nine blocks. Fill in the order below (Customer Segments first, Unfair Advantage last), because later blocks depend on earlier ones.

For each block: what it captures, what "good" looks like, and the trap to avoid.

## 1. Customer Segments (+ Early Adopters)
- **Captures:** The specific group whose problem you're solving. Within it, the **Early Adopters** — the subset who feel the problem most acutely *today*.
- **Good:** Named narrowly enough that you could go find ten of them this week. Creators vs. consumers separated if your model has both sides.
- **Trap:** "Everyone," or a segment so broad it has no shared problem. If you can't picture one real person in it, it's too wide. Early adopters are not "tech enthusiasts" by default — they're whoever has the most pain.

## 2. Problem (+ Existing Alternatives)
- **Captures:** The top 1–3 problems for that segment. **Existing Alternatives** = how they solve these *today* (often a workaround, a competitor, or "email / a spreadsheet / nothing").
- **Good:** Problems stated from the customer's point of view, ranked. Existing alternatives are concrete and current — they're the anchor customers will judge your price and value against.
- **Trap:** Listing your solution's features as "problems." Inventing problems you assume rather than ones customers voice. Forgetting that "do nothing" and free tools are alternatives you must beat.

## 3. Unique Value Proposition (+ High-Level Concept)
- **Captures:** A single, clear, compelling sentence stating why you're different and worth buying — aimed at the early adopter, anchored on the #1 problem and the finished benefit. The **High-Level Concept** is a separate X-for-Y analogy ("YouTube for X", "Flickr for video") that makes the idea instantly graspable.
- **Good:** Specific, outcome-focused, uses the customer's words (which you harvest from problem interviews). Distinct from the high-level concept — the UVP sells, the analogy explains.
- **Trap:** Vague adjectives ("fast, easy, powerful"), feature lists, jargon. A UVP you wrote before talking to anyone is a guess — refine it with the language customers actually use.

## 4. Solution
- **Captures:** The *smallest* set of features that addresses each top problem. Bind each solution element to a specific problem.
- **Good:** Sketch-level, not a spec. One bullet per top problem. Deliberately minimal — this becomes your MVP.
- **Trap:** This is the founder's favorite box and the one to keep smallest (it's one-ninth of the canvas for a reason). Don't design the whole product here; bind features to validated problems only.

## 5. Channels
- **Captures:** Your path(s) to reach customers — inbound/outbound, free/paid, direct/indirect.
- **Good:** Channels you can actually start using at your current scale; a plan for finding more early adopters like the ones you have.
- **Trap:** "Viral / word of mouth" as a hope rather than a mechanism. Channels that only work at scale you don't have yet. Building a path to customers is often *harder* than building the product — take it seriously.

## 6. Revenue Streams
- **Captures:** How you make money — model (one-time, subscription, freemium, marketplace fee…), price, and lifetime value.
- **Good:** A concrete price you'd actually charge, from day one. Pricing is part of the product and a strong signal of value.
- **Trap:** "We'll figure out monetization later" / "ads." Free-forever with no path to revenue. Underpricing because you're afraid to charge — price is a hypothesis you test like any other.

## 7. Cost Structure
- **Captures:** The costs to operate and to reach your launch — customer acquisition cost, hosting/infrastructure, people, fixed costs.
- **Good:** Enough to compute a rough **break-even point** (how many customers to cover costs) and to see whether the margins work.
- **Trap:** Forgetting customer acquisition cost (often the dominant cost). Ignoring your own time. A model that only works at implausible scale.

## 8. Key Metrics
- **Captures:** The few numbers that tell you how the business is doing — ideally the steps of your customer lifecycle (acquisition → activation → retention → revenue → referral).
- **Good:** A small set tied to the value you deliver; one you'd check weekly. Maps to the metrics `measure-fit` will track.
- **Trap:** Vanity metrics (page views, total signups) that go up while the business goes nowhere. Tracking everything, focusing on nothing.

## 9. Unfair Advantage
- **Captures:** Something that can't be easily copied or bought — insider information, an authority/personal brand, a dream team, an existing network, a community, real switching costs.
- **Good:** Honest. A real moat, or blank.
- **Trap:** Wishful entries — "passion," "first-mover advantage," "great features," "hard work." These are not unfair advantages. It is completely fine to leave this blank early; most ventures earn their advantage later. Don't fill it to feel better.

## Filling order, restated

`Customer Segments → Problem → UVP (+ High-Level Concept) → Solution → Channels → Revenue → Cost → Key Metrics → Unfair Advantage`. Earlier blocks constrain later ones; the segment determines the problem, which determines the UVP and solution, and the segment + model determine channels and pricing.
