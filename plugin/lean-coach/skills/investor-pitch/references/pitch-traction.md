# The pitch & traction

Detail reference for the `investor-pitch` skill. Grounded in *Running Lean*'s stance on funding and traction.

## Traction is the pitch

> "Traction is a measure of your product's engagement with its market. Investors care about traction over everything else." — Nivi & Naval, *Venture Hacks* (quoted in *Running Lean*)

The defining Lean idea about fundraising: **lead with traction, not the solution.** Founders love to open with the product; investors (Dave McClure's observation in the book) repeatedly see founders spend too long on the solution and not enough on the rest of the model. Customers — and investors — identify with *problems*, not your features. So the pitch order that lands is roughly:

1. **Traction / why-now** — the evidence that this is working and that now is the moment. Signups that *retain*, paying customers, growth rate, a sharp wedge.
2. **Problem** — the must-have problem, for a specific segment, and how they cope today (existing alternatives).
3. **Solution** — the smallest thing that delivers the UVP. Brief.
4. **Market** — big enough, and *reachable* through your channels (size alone isn't enough).
5. **Business model** — how you make money; unit economics.
6. **Team / unfair advantage** — why you, why you'll outlearn competitors.
7. **The ask** — a specific amount tied to specific milestones.

A traction-first deck inverts the instinct to save the numbers for the end. If you have real traction, it's your strongest card — open with it.

## When to raise

From the methodology (`../../../reference/methodology.md`): the **ideal time to raise a big round is after product/market fit**, because then your goal and the investor's are the same — growth. Before PM fit, your goal is *learning* and the investor's is *growth*; the misalignment makes raising harder and the money can buy premature scaling (a form of waste). A small runway to fund validation can make sense earlier — Bootstrapping + Lean = a low-burn startup that delays or shrinks the raise. Don't pitch a months-long business plan / 10-slide deck built on untested hypotheses.

## The numbers an investor will probe

Know these cold; vagueness reads as "hasn't built anything real":

- **Traction metrics** — activation and **retention** (the real macro — are users coming back?), not just signups.
- **Growth rate** — week-over-week / month-over-month.
- **CAC** (cost to acquire a customer) and the **channels** that produce it.
- **LTV** (lifetime value); rule of thumb **LTV > 3 × CAC**.
- **Churn / retention** — and which **engine of growth** you're running (sticky / viral / paid — see `../../measure-fit/references/product-market-fit.md`).
- **Market size** — and, more pointedly, *how you reach it affordably*.

## Common investor attacks (and what a good answer looks like)

| Attack | Weak answer | Strong answer |
|---|---|---|
| "These are just signups." | "We have 5,000 users!" | "1,200 activated, 40% retained month over month — here's the cohort." |
| "How do you reach customers?" | "Word of mouth / it'll go viral." | A working channel with a real CAC, plus the next channel being tested. |
| "Who are your competitors?" | "We have none." | The existing alternatives (including "do nothing" and free tools) and why you beat them. |
| "What's your riskiest assumption?" | deflection | Names it (from `risks.md`) and shows the experiment that's testing it. |
| "Why now / why you?" | generic passion | A specific shift in the world + a real unfair advantage. |
| "How much and what for?" | "Raising a round." | "$X for 12 months to hit <specific milestone>." |

The strongest currency throughout is **evidence**: cohorts, conversion numbers, customer quotes, a working channel. A pitch is a risk-mitigation story told with data, not a vision told with adjectives.
