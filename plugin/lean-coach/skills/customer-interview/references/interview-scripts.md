# Customer interview scripts

Detail reference for the `customer-interview` skill. Three scripts from *Running Lean* (Ch. 7 Problem Interview, Ch. 8 Solution Interview, Ch. 11 MVP Interview), plus the universal do's & don'ts. Times are guides for a ~30-minute conversation; the exact wording adapts to the venture's canvas.

## Universal rules (all flavors)

- **Learning, not pitching.** The interview is to learn, not to sell. Devote ~20% to setup, ~80% to listening.
- **Don't lead.** Ask how they do things today; don't ask whether they'd use your thing. Never ask customers what they want — watch what they do and have done.
- **Talk to strangers, a few degrees out.** Friends and family give rosy, unreliable signals; use them to rehearse the script, not as data.
- **Run with two people when you can** — one talks, one takes notes — and document within 5 minutes while it's fresh. If solo, take notes immediately after.
- **Get out of the building.** In person or by call beats surveys; surveys verify, they don't teach.

## Problem interview (Ch. 7) — tests the problem-customer fit

Risks tackled: *Product (what are you solving?)*, *Market (who's the competition / existing alternatives?)*, *Customer (who has the pain?)*. Script structure:

| Step | Time | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Welcome | 2 min | Set the stage |
| 2. Collect demographics | 2 min | Test/qualify the customer segment; identify early adopters |
| 3. Tell a story | 2 min | Set the problem context |
| 4. Problem ranking | 4 min | Present top 1–3 problems; have them rank |
| 5. Explore customer's worldview | 15 min | The heart — how do they solve this today? |
| 6. Wrapping up | 2 min | The hook + ask (permission to follow up, referrals) |
| 7. Document results | 5 min | Capture while fresh |

- **Welcome:** thank them; say what you're working on and that you want to *learn from* them, not sell. "We don't have a finished product; our goal is to learn from you."
- **Demographics:** a few questions that drive how you'll segment/qualify early adopters (for a parent-photo product: how many kids, ages, do you share photos/videos online, how often, with whom).
- **Tell a story:** illustrate the top problems with a short narrative, then ask "does any of this resonate with you?"
- **Problem ranking:** state the top 1–3 problems, ask them to rank; ask for any pet peeves you missed. Reorder the list between interviews so you don't bias the ranking.
- **Explore worldview (the heart — "the best script here is no script"):** for each problem, ask how they address it *today*, then sit back and listen. Don't lead. Watch body language for must-have / nice-to-have / don't-need. Probe the disconnect: if they call something a must-have but do nothing about it, that's a signal it isn't. ("How do you share photos/videos today? Walk me through your workflow. What do you use, and how did you find it?")
- **Wrapping up:** give a **hook** (a high-level concept pitch — a memorable sound bite) to keep interest without pitching the solution; ask permission to follow up; ask for **referrals** to others like them.
- **Exit criteria:** ≥10 interviews and you can identify an early adopter's demographics, have a must-have problem, and can describe how customers solve it today.

## Solution interview (Ch. 8) — tests the solution

Once you have a must-have problem and know the existing alternatives, demo the *smallest* solution (a mock-up/sketch, not built software) and test whether it excites early adopters and whether they'll pay. Structure:

| Step | Time | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Welcome | 2 min | Set the stage |
| 2. Collect demographics | 2 min | Confirm they're an early adopter |
| 3. Tell a story | 2 min | Re-anchor on the top problems |
| 4. Demo | 8 min | Walk a mock-up that addresses each top problem; watch reaction |
| 5. Test price | 3 min | State a price; gauge the reaction (don't ask "would you pay?" — name a number) |
| 6. Wrapping up | 2 min | Ask for the order / permission to follow up; referrals |
| 7. Document results | 5 min | Capture while fresh |

- Bind each demo element to a problem you validated; skip features that don't map to a top problem.
- **Test price by stating it** and reading the reaction — silence, a flinch, "that's reasonable." The strongest signal is a commitment (pre-order, a credit card, a clear "yes, when can I have it").
- Identify the early adopters who'd rather *buy* your solution than build their own.
- **Exit criteria:** early adopters who want the solution, a price they accept, and a defined **Release 1.0 (MVP)** = the minimum feature set to deliver the UVP.

## MVP interview (Ch. 11) — tests the live MVP, usability-test style

With the MVP, marketing page, and a conversion dashboard ready, you visit warm prospects to sign them up and, in the process, test messaging, pricing, and the activation flow. Run it as a **usability test** (think-aloud), not a pitch. Structure:

| Step | Time | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Welcome | 2 min | Set the stage; "think out loud as we go" |
| 2. Show landing page | 2 min | Test UVP — is it clear what this is? what would you do next? |
| 3. Show pricing page | 3 min | Test pricing — let them reach it, then ask what they think |
| 4. Signup & activation | 15 min | The heart — watch them sign up and complete the key activity |
| 5. Wrapping up | 2 min | Keep the feedback loop open; permission to follow up |
| 6. Document results | 5 min | Top usability problems; willingness to pay |

- Run it like Steve Krug's usability testing (*Rocket Surgery Made Easy*): ask them to think aloud, don't help, note where they hesitate or fail. "If you can't convert a warm prospect in a 20-minute face-to-face, it'll be far harder to convert a stranger in 8 seconds on your landing page."
- Tests: *Product (is the UVP compelling? does the MVP deliver it?)*, *Customer (can channels drive enough signups?)*, *Market (is the price right — do they pay?)*.
- **Exit criteria:** warm prospects sign up and get through activation; usability hot spots identified; pricing reaction captured.

## What to do with results

Review interviews **weekly** (don't change the script mid-week). Each week: home in on the most-favorable demographics (your early adopters), drop the least-favorable segments, refine the problem list (drop "don't-need," add new "must-haves"), really understand existing alternatives (your value/price must beat them — including "free" and "do nothing"), and harvest the **words customers use** for your UVP. Fold the learning back into the canvas (`lean-canvas`).
